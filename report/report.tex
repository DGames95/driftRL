\section{Vehicle model}
The car is a 3-DOF single-track (bicycle) model with body-frame states
$(v_x, v_y, r)$ and global pose $(x, y, \psi)$:
\begin{align}
\dot{v}_x &= \tfrac{1}{m}\left(F_x - F_{y,f}\sin\delta\right) + r\,v_y, &
\dot{v}_y &= \tfrac{1}{m}\left(F_{y,f}\cos\delta + F_{y,r}\right) - r\,v_x, \\
\dot{r}  &= \tfrac{1}{I_z}\left(l_f F_{y,f}\cos\delta - l_r F_{y,r}\right), &
\dot{\psi} &= r,
\end{align}
with $\dot{x} = v_x\cos\psi - v_y\sin\psi$ and
$\dot{y} = v_x\sin\psi + v_y\cos\psi$.
Lateral tire forces are linear with $\tanh$ saturation,
\begin{equation}
F_{y,i} = -F_{y,\max,i}\,\tanh\!\left(\frac{C_{\alpha,i}\,\alpha_i}{F_{y,\max,i}}\right),
\qquad
\alpha_f = \arctan\!\frac{v_y + l_f r}{v_x} - \delta,
\quad
\alpha_r = \arctan\!\frac{v_y - l_r r}{v_x},
\end{equation}
where $F_{y,\max,i} = \mu F_{z,i}$ uses the static axle loads. The throttle
command produces a rear-axle longitudinal force capped at the friction
limit, $F_{x,r} = \mathrm{clip}(T\,F_{\mathrm{drive,max}}, \pm\mu F_{z,r})$,
and a friction-ellipse coupling shrinks the rear lateral limit as
longitudinal force is used:
\begin{equation}
F_{y,\max,r}^{\mathrm{eff}} = F_{y,\max,r}
\sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{F_{x,r}}{\mu F_{z,r}}\right)^{2}}.
\label{eq:ellipse}
\end{equation}
This coupling is what makes the model \emph{driftable}: opening the
throttle directly removes rear lateral grip (power-oversteer). Total
longitudinal force adds quadratic drag, $F_x = F_{x,r} - c_d v_x |v_x|$,
and integration is explicit Euler with $\Delta t = 0.02$\,s.

\paragraph{Why drifting is hard to control.}
Figure~\ref{fig:instability} shows the open-loop sensitivity introduced by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ellipse}. Constant inputs ($\delta = 0.09$, $T = 0.10$)
corner the 30\,m circle steadily for the full simulated horizon. Adding a
single 0.5\,s full-throttle pulse to the otherwise identical run collapses
the rear lateral limit, and the car departs to a slip angle of
$83^\circ$ --- a spin --- within 1.5\,s, with no further input change.
Beyond the grip limit the dynamics are open-loop unstable, which is what
makes sustained drifting a genuinely closed-loop control problem.

\section{Environment}
\texttt{DriftEnv} (Gymnasium) exposes the observation
$[v_x, v_y, r, e_y, e_\psi, \kappa_0, \kappa_{10}, \kappa_{25}]$:
body velocities and yaw rate, signed lateral offset from the track
centerline ($e_y > 0$ left), heading error to the local tangent, and the
centerline curvature 0, 10 and 25\,m ahead. Actions are
$[\delta, T]$, $\delta \in [-0.5, 0.5]$\,rad, $T \in [-1, 1]$.

Two track types share the same sampled-centerline machinery
(\texttt{track.py}): a circle of radius 30\,m, and random open tracks of
500\,m whose curvature is a piecewise-linear profile through random knots
spaced 30--80\,m apart with magnitudes bounded in
$[1/60, 1/22]\,\mathrm{m}^{-1}$ (corner radii 22--60\,m) and random sign.
Random tracks regenerate every episode.

Episodes start at 8\,m/s on the centerline, run at most 1000 steps (20\,s),
and terminate with an additional $-400$ reward if $|e_y| > 4$\,m or
$v_x < 1$\,m/s. The per-step reward shares a base term and differs only in
how slip is treated:
\begin{equation}
R = v_x\cos e_\psi - 0.5\,e_y^2 - 0.002\,\dot{\delta}^2 +
\begin{cases}
+3.0\,|\beta| & \text{drift mode},\\[2pt]
-50\,\beta^2 & \text{grip mode},
\end{cases}
\qquad \beta = \arctan\frac{v_y}{v_x}.
\end{equation}

Both agents are trained with Stable-Baselines3 PPO (\texttt{MlpPolicy},
$3\times10^{-4}$ learning rate, $\gamma = 0.999$, otherwise defaults) for
500\,000 steps on the circular track; an \texttt{EvalCallback} keeps the
best checkpoint. The discount factor is raised from the default 0.99
because the shorter value horizon let a full-throttle policy that leaves
the track after 3.7\,s appear optimal.

\section{Grip baseline: validating the physics}
\label{sec:grip}
In grip mode the slip penalty makes the optimal behavior plain fast
driving, which has a known answer to compare against. In steady-state
cornering at radius $R$ both axles saturate simultaneously (the static
load split makes the yaw moment balance exactly), and the rear must also
supply the drag force through Eq.~\eqref{eq:ellipse}, giving
\begin{equation}
v_{\max}^4\left[\left(\frac{m}{R}\right)^2 +
\left(\frac{c_d L}{l_f}\right)^2\right] = (\mu m g)^2
\;\;\Rightarrow\;\;
v_{\max} = 16.26\;\mathrm{m/s} \;\;\text{at } R = 30\,\mathrm{m},
\end{equation}
within 0.1\% of the drag-free $\sqrt{\mu g R} = 16.27$\,m/s.

The trained grip agent laps the circle for the full 20\,s episode
(return 13001) with the slip angle never exceeding $7.4^\circ$.
Longitudinal velocity settles at 14.8\,m/s and peaks at 15.7\,m/s,
approaching but never exceeding the analytic limit --- the learned policy
recovers the traction limit of the model to within $\sim$9\% (settled)
without being told it.

\section{Drift agent}
With the slip reward, the learned behavior changes qualitatively. Across
eight evaluation seeds, six episodes run the full 20\,s with returns
between 11\,600 and 12\,400 and settled mean slip angles of
$5.7$--$9.4^\circ$ (the remaining two leave the track at 13.0 and 15.0\,s).
The representative episode shown (seed 3, return 12419) holds a settled
mean slip angle of $7.6^\circ$ with peaks of $20.4^\circ$ at
14.1\,m/s average speed over 1.5 laps. The throttle is actively modulated
between 0.11 and 0.85 (mean 0.38) rather than held constant --- the agent
uses the friction-ellipse coupling of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ellipse} as a control
input. The phase portrait (Figure~\ref{fig:phase}) shows the slip is
sustained as a recurring drift--catch--drift cycle, with steering swinging
repeatedly to the $+0.5$\,rad lock, rather than a steady-state drift
equilibrium.

\section{Manual play}
The same environment is playable as a top-down game (\texttt{game.py},
pygame, 50\,Hz real-time). The camera is a chase view aligned with the
velocity vector, so the angle between the car body and screen-up is the
slip angle. Arrow keys command steering and throttle through first-order
lags ($\tau = 0.12$\,s and $0.25$\,s), mimicking analog inputs. Both track
types are playable; random tracks regenerate on restart, and the trained
drift agent can drive in the same view (\texttt{--demo}) for side-by-side
comparison. The qualitative behaviors of Section~\ref{sec:grip} are
reproducible by hand: lifting or stabbing the throttle mid-corner visibly
rotates the car, holding it spins out, and modest countersteer catches the
slide.
